## 5. The "did anything surprise me" slot

> _The thing you'd otherwise lose between now and 6D. Could be a vocabulary choice, an example, a tension, a place where Leveson's argument felt thinner than the orientation predicted, or a place where it felt sharper. One paragraph, raw._

CAST is structurally more open than STPA, and the openness is not stylistic. STPA takes a finite specification (system + hazards) and produces artifacts under fixed taxonomies — four UCA types, mechanical UCA→CSC dualisation, two scenario classes — so it can be procedural. CAST takes a trajectory σ as input and *explains* it; explanations don't tabulate the way hypothetical futures do, so the per-component template stays open. Forward reasoning generates, backward reasoning selects — and selection requires judgment (this is why Leveson keeps policing hindsight bias; STPA needs no such policing because you can't be biased about a future you're constructing). The deepest reason is the taxonomy gap: STPA's four UCA types are the four projections of F's output (`rem:uca-observation`), enumerable because F is fixed; CAST asks "where did γ depart from γ\*?", and the shape of that departure depends on γ\*, which depends on the system — no finite taxonomy of process-model flaws is possible. **Consequence:** the proximal event chain (§11.2) is the only procedural moment in CAST, and it is exactly the place where the classical chain camp briefly returns. Chain causality is algebraic/inductive (mechanical); STAMP is coalgebraic/observational (open). CAST inherits its openness from the model it operationalizes. **For 6D:** this is the structural reason the forward/backward γ pair cannot be presented as symmetric — the backward direction is judgment-laden in a way the forward direction is not.
